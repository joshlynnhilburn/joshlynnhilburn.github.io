    • Affordable rental housing in Chicago

  • I. Introduction

    I analyzed open government data published by the City of Chicago that details affordable rental housing developments in order to make this information clear and detailed for those who would find it useful. I conducted data cleaning, processing, and analysis as well as created visualizations to explore this dataset.

    II. Data Cleaning

    The data is composed of 488 rows organized into 14 columns, which primarily includes information about neighborhoods, property types, property names, addresses, zip codes, unit numbers, and coordinates. Data cleaning was primarily conducted in Microsoft Excel, starting with the removal of duplicates and blank cells then systematically using pivot tables to see fields organized by content. To see a full detailed log of my cleaning process, go here. After all cleaning and processing was completed, the dataset was organized by neighborhood with all inaccurate and blank fields addressed accordingly; the final dataset contains 487 rows and was now ready for analysis.

    III. Data Analysis

    To analyze data, I used pivot tables to combine the data in varying ways in order to reveal trends and relationships. Within the cleaned data, 487 affordable rental housing developments in Chicago are supported by city programs to maintain affordability in local neighborhoods. There are 64 different neighborhoods represented in 52 zip codes, and all properties are bounded by a rectangle composed of the coordinates (41.64845741, -87.80707301), (41.64845741, -87.54012317), (42.01714971, -87.80707301), (42.01714971, -87.54012317) which are represented by dots on the graphic above.

    Approximately half (49.49%, or 241 of 487) of housing developments are located in only 9 communities: Humboldt Park, North Lawndale, West Town, Logan Square, Grand Boulevard, Uptown, Near West Side, Oakland and Woodlawn. The other 55 communities make up the other half of the rental properties. This shows that the first neighborhoods one should be apartment hunting in should be these 9 because they are the most numerous for that within the dataset. 14.06% (or 9 of 64) communities comprise half of the developments in the dataset.

    A similar analysis was conducted for rental developments across zip codes. Approximately half (51.95% or 253 of 487) of affordable rental housing developments are located within 8 zip codes: 60647, 60653, 60608, 60624, 60637, 60640, 60622, 60612. Potential residents seeking low-cost apartments should prioritize looking in these locations, because most affordable housing developments will be located there.

    This pie chart shows the breakdown of rental developments by property time; as expected, the biggest share is of Multifamily-style housing meant for a familial unit. Senior properties and ARO (Affordable Requirements Ordinance) are the next biggest part while Artist, Disabled, SRO, Supportive, and Veterans housing make up the remaining 10.06% of housing. That means about 90% of housing is designated for Seniors, Families, and are ARO, which is the bulk of the population demographic within Chicago.

    This bar chart shows how many communities have at least one of the specific property type in them. As shown above, Senior-type properties are distributed across 52 of the 64 different communities, while Artist, Disabled, SRO, and Veterans housing is only in 1, 2, or 3 different communities. Senior housing is the most widespread while Multifamily housing comes in a close second at distribution in 33 communities. Those looking for Senior and Multifamily-specific housing have a much wider variety of neighborhoods to choose from than those looking for more niche types of housing.

    Within both communities and zip codes, the most dominant type of property type is displayed above in a stacked bar chart. Again, Senior-type housing is the most dominant kind followed by Multifamily and ARO housing. Even though there are more Multifamily units in the dataset by a lot, they are less widespread and distributed than Senior housing which is less concentrated. In no community did Veterans, SRO, or Disabled make up the most dominant kind. There were more than twice the number of multifamily properties but they were less widespread and more concentrated compared to senior.

    Finally, this analysis shows the locational distribution of all properties in the dataset. 50% of properties are above the latitude 41.8658847 and 50% of properties are east of the longitude -87.66511109. The average coordinate for the locations of all properties in this dataset is (41.86734214, -87.6669266). The mean for the latitude is slightly lower than the median and the mean for the longitude is slightly higher than the median. Latitude is skewed slightly lower on average but longitude is slightly higher (more east) on average.

    IV. Data Visualizations

    To make data visualizations, I used the chart tools in Microsoft Excel as well as Data Studio to make an interactive dashboard. All static graphs made in Excel are above while the dashboard can be viewed here. This dashboard can be controlled by the neighborhood. Rental housing developments in this dataset include listed the thousands of affordable units that supported by City of Chicago programs to maintain affordability in local neighborhoods. The list is updated when construction is completed for new projects or when the compliance period for older projects expire.

    V. Conclusion

    In short, affordable rental housing in Chicago is necessary to house the low-income population of the city. Resources such as this data report make it clear and easier for those looking for housing to make a decision, based off of their specific needs and wants in terms of property type and location.
